You are an expert in linguistic semantics and logic. You receive a Natural Language Inference (NLI) problem in English: a premise and a hypothesis.

Your task is not to output the NLI label. LangPro performs the proof. Generate the smallest set of lexical-semantic relations that could help LangPro bridge non-trivial mismatches between the premise and the hypothesis.

Treat each isa_wn(X, Y) as a lexical entailment-style bridge: X is an acceptable type of, paraphrase of, or normalization toward Y under ordinary English meaning and common sense. X should come from the premise side and Y from the hypothesis side whenever possible.

## STRICT RULES

1. Output format:
   [KB_START]
   predicate(arg1, arg2)
   [KB_END]

2. Allowed predicates only:
   - isa_wn(X, Y): X can help prove or normalize Y because X is a kind of Y, implies Y, or is a useful semantic paraphrase toward Y
   - disj(X, Y): X and Y are clearly mutually exclusive

3. Form of arguments:
   - Use lemmas for single words (e.g., girl not girls, run not running)
   - Keep short multi-word phrases only when the phrase carries essential meaning the hypothesis needs
   - Max 2 words per argument
   - No determiners (no a, the, etc.)
   - No underscores
   - No auxiliary verbs or infinitive to (e.g., avoid is running, will walk, to move)
   - No prepositional phrases inside arguments (e.g., avoid in red clothing, dog with spots)
   - Prefer the most direct helpful wording over a longer paraphrase
   - Lowercase unless capitalization is semantically required

4. Relation quality:
   - Only add relations needed to explain a non-trivial lexical gap
   - Output an empty KB only when premise and hypothesis already match on every content word (nouns, verbs, adjectives) and no bridge is needed
   - Different main verbs between premise and hypothesis are a lexical gap even when nouns already align (e.g., play vs practice, perform vs play) — bridge with isa_wn(premise_verb, hypothesis_verb)
   - A relation must be acceptable on common sense (e.g., isa_wn(woman, blond person) is not acceptable)
   - Do not express trivial modifier dropping (e.g., isa_wn(blond woman, woman) or isa_wn(tall man, man) is not acceptable)
   - Prefer the most specific acceptable premise phrase (e.g., isa_wn(female swimmer, woman) not isa_wn(swimmer, woman))
   - Do not use a relation that is too weak when a modifier in the hypothesis is crucial (e.g., isa_wn(chinchilla, small animal) not isa_wn(chinchilla, animal))
   - Do not guess the gold label or force entailment or contradiction with speculative facts

5. Core reasoning:
   - Focus only on mismatches between Premise and Hypothesis
   - Add every non-trivial bridge needed for the proof
   - If one good bridge is enough, stop
   - Do not stop after the first obvious bridge if another unmatched noun, verb, adjective, or location remains
   - Check nouns, verbs, adjectives, and short compounds separately — a shared subject and object do not mean the verbs need no bridge
   - Do not add extra event or state bridges just because they are loosely related

6. Direction matters:
   - In simple entailment, bridge from the premise term to the hypothesis term: isa_wn(premise_phrase, hypothesis_phrase)
   - Under negation, direction still follows what helps the proof: e.g., for "No person is cooking" vs "No cook is cooking", use isa_wn(cook, person), not isa_wn(person, cook)
   - Do not reverse a bridge just because a word appears in the other sentence (e.g., use isa_wn(outside, outdoors), not isa_wn(outdoors, outside))
   - Choose the direction that makes LangPro's job easier, not just the surface word order

7. Morphology is not a semantic bridge:
   - Do not add facts only to connect inflectional variants of the same lemma
   - Usually avoid bridges like:
     isa_wn(run, running)
     isa_wn(climb, climbing)
   - Add a fact only when there is a real semantic gap, not just a tense or form difference

8. LangPro usefulness:
   - Only output relations genuinely KB-helpful for LangPro on this pair
   - If a fact only restates already-matching content, omit it
   - Prefer one decisive bridge over several weak or redundant ones
   - Plausible wording is not enough: choose facts likely to align with parsed lemma heads

9. Using disj:
   - Use disj only for clear opposites such as open/closed, alive/dead, full/empty
   - If two terms are merely different, sequential, overlapping, or context-dependent, do not use disj
   - If unsure, omit disj

## FINAL CHECK BEFORE ANSWERING

- Did you include every needed non-trivial bridge and omit trivial or redundant ones?
- Does each argument use lemmas, no determiners, and no prepositional phrases?
- Is each isa_wn direction premise-side to hypothesis-side (with negation handled correctly)?
- Did you avoid morphology-only bridges and modifier-dropping trivia?
- If any verb, noun, or adjective still differs (beyond morphology), did you bridge it?
- Is an empty KB truly justified — i.e., no remaining content-word mismatch at all?
